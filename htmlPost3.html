MINOR SCALES

Previously we looked at major scale and how it can be viewed from the Pentachord system point of view. Today we'd look at minor scales, unique characteristics and variations. The natural minor scale is a diatonic scale that is built on a specific pattern of whole steps (W) and half steps (H): W-H-W-W-H-W-W. This results in a scale with a different sound and character compared to the major scale. Eg. In the key of A minor, the natural minor scale consists of the following notes: A-B-C-D-E-F-G-A. The natural minor scale is often associated with a more melancholic or somber mood compared to the major scale. By using the interval pattern for minor scales we can see more minor scales starting from different notes (tonal centers; we'll discuss this in subsequent lessons) Apart from the natural minor scale, there are also other variations of the minor scale, each with its own unique characteristics:

1. Harmonic minor scale: The harmonic minor scale is similar to the natural minor scale, but with a raised seventh degree (#7). This alteration creates a larger interval between the sixth and seventh degrees of the scale, which gives it a distinctive sound. For example, in the key of A harmonic minor, the scale consists of the notes: A-B-C-D-E-F-G#-A. The raised seventh degree allows for a leading tone, which can create a stronger sense of resolution when used in melodies and harmonies.

2. Melodic minor scale: The melodic minor scale is another variation of the minor scale that has different ascending and descending forms. In the ascending form, the sixth and seventh degrees are raised (#6 and #7) compared to the natural minor scale, while the descending form returns to the natural minor scale. E.g. In the key of A melodic minor, the ascending scale consists of the notes: A-B-C-D-E-F#-G#-A, while the descending scale returns to the natural minor scale: A-G-F-E-D-C-B-A. The melodic minor scale is often used in classical and jazz music to create interesting melodic lines and harmonies.
